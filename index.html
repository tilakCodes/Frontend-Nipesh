Plastic Fiber Reinforced Mortar Study
Research — 8th Semester
Project Scope

A comprehensive 8th semester research project investigating the use of recycled plastic fibers as reinforcement in cement mortar mixes. The study aimed to evaluate improvements in flexural and shear strength while assessing long-term durability characteristics, targeting cost-effective and sustainable construction solutions suitable for Nepal's building industry.

Methodology & Software Used

Laboratory-based experimental approach involving systematic preparation of mortar specimens with varying plastic fiber concentrations (0.5%, 1.0%, 1.5% by volume). Compression, flexural, and split-tensile tests were performed per IS standards. Data analysis was conducted using Microsoft Excel for statistical comparison against control specimens. AutoCAD was used for specimen layout documentation.

Key Technical Outcome

Results demonstrated a measurable increase in flexural strength (up to 18%) at optimal fiber dosage, with improved crack-resistance and post-cracking ductility. The research provided practical recommendations for incorporating recycled plastic fibers in low-cost mortar applications, contributing to sustainable construction practices in developing regions.

Seismic Failure Analysis: Jajarkot Earthquake
Analysis — 5th Semester
Project Scope

A 5th semester field-study and analytical project examining the structural failure patterns observed during the Jajarkot earthquake. The project assessed the causes of building collapse in masonry and reinforced concrete structures, focusing on design deficiencies, construction malpractices, and material quality issues prevalent in rural Nepalese construction.

Methodology & Software Used

Methodology combined on-site visual damage assessment with desktop analysis. Field surveys documented failure modes (soft-storey collapse, column shear failure, joint failures) with photographic evidence. Structural vulnerability was evaluated using ETABS modelling for representative building typologies. Reference was made to NBC 105 (Nepal Building Code) and IS 1893 seismic provisions.

Key Technical Outcome

The analysis identified critical vulnerabilities including inadequate lateral force resisting systems, poor detailing of beam-column joints, and absence of seismic bands in masonry structures. Recommendations included retrofit strategies (jacketing, FRP wrapping) and revised construction guidelines for seismic Zone V compliance, contributing to disaster-resilient building practices in Western Nepal.

Bio-Engineering Applications in Nepal
Infrastructure — 3rd Semester
Project Scope

A 3rd semester fieldwork project exploring the application of bio-engineering techniques for slope stabilization and erosion control in Nepal's mountainous terrain. The study evaluated vegetative and structural interventions suitable for road-cut slopes, riverbanks, and landslide-prone areas across the mid-hill region.

Methodology & Software Used

Fieldwork-based methodology including site reconnaissance, soil sampling, and slope profiling. Bio-engineering interventions evaluated included vetiver grass planting, jute netting, live staking (Napier grass, Amriso), and brush layering. Slope stability was assessed using manual calculations with reference to DSCWM (Department of Soil Conservation) guidelines. Site mapping utilized basic surveying instruments and ArcGIS for geospatial documentation.

Key Technical Outcome

The project demonstrated that combining vegetative measures with lightweight civil structures (check dams, gabion walls) provided the most cost-effective and ecologically sustainable solution for slope stabilization. Findings recommended specific plant species and installation techniques suited to different altitude zones of Nepal, contributing to the Department of Roads' bio-engineering implementation framework.
